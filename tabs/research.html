Engineering enzyme reactivity

How do enzymes function, and how can we modify their behavior?

Enzymes are a diverse class of proteins that catalyze many of the biochemical reactions on which life is dependent. To this end, enzymes are very well adapted for their functions and often exhibit high degrees of specificity and efficiency, characteristics that make them attractive catalysts in a variety of applications.

However, in many cases, natural enzymes are highly specialized for their native function, and it can be difficult to adapt them toward catalytic activity on a new substrate (changing their specificity) or toward improved catalytic activity on an existing substrate. To expand beyond these limitations, many researchers have introduced methods for re-engineering enzyme function, but there is still much room for improvement in these approaches.

Under Bruce Tidor, my work in graduate school has been focused on developing methods for redesigning an enzyme for increased activity on an existing substrate. Currently, most methods primarily focus on the energetic gap between the enzyme-bound ground state and transition state, without explicit consideration of the enzyme-substrate complex's dynamical approach toward, and along, the energy surface connecting these two important states. The methods I've developed in graduate school capture and use this information, relate it to enzyme activity, and apply it to assist in enzyme redesign for enhanced activity. We will be publishing our results very soon.
